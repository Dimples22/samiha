A Guide To My Brain

Published Mar 12, 2026

Content Warning: I mention BPD in this essay because I have researched it for years and believe I meet the criteria. This is not based on TikTok or trends, and I am not claiming a formal diagnosis. I have been seen by CAMHS, who did not want to give a diagnosis as a minor but noted pre-developmental trauma leading to low moods and anxiety. I did CBT, which did not help much, and wanted to do DBT, but that requires a diagnosis, as my CAMHS therapist mentioned. After experiencing a manic episode associated with bipolar following sertraline, my GP said I could pursue a bipolar assessment, though I did not go through it. I am aware that some experiences are part of bipolar and not BPD, and it is possible to have both, but some terms are also used colloquially, like “manic” in BPD, which is not clinical but can describe feelings. Others have observed symptoms of BPD as well as autism or ADHD, though CAMHS did not think I had these, and masking may have made diagnosis difficult. I mention BPD because researching it and seeing how others with similar experiences describe it helps me process why I am the way I am and provides comfort. This is about understanding myself, not about trends or seeking attention, and not to invite judgment or assumptions.

Introduction

Some behaviours that I once viewed as personal flaws can be better understood as symptoms associated with Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD) and trauma-related changes in emotional regulation. Research in clinical psychology shows that prolonged trauma can change how the brain processes threat, relationships, and emotions. Changes in areas such as the amygdala, hippocampus, and prefrontal cortex can affect emotional regulation, impulse control, and threat perception [1][2]. These shifts can make reactions feel automatic or difficult to control.

People often say that mental illness is not an excuse for behaviour. Responsibility still exists, especially when harm is done. However, symptoms can help explain why certain responses occur and why they may feel disproportionate or difficult to regulate in the moment. Furthermore, some situations are more nuanced, and no one may truly be the one “in the wrong.” In such cases, using the term “excuse” can feel harmful and demonising.

Over time, I have tried to understand these patterns through both reflection and research. Although diagnosis usually comes from mental health professionals, I have spent significant time reading psychological literature and comparing it with my own experiences. My experiences do not fit perfectly within a single diagnostic category, and this is true for everyone who receives a diagnosis. Not everyone experiences the same disorder in the same way, and it does not make anyone better or worse. Instead, my experiences often reflect an overlap between patterns associated with BPD and mood changes that resemble aspects of the bipolar spectrum.

Another complication is that much of this struggle is not always visible to others. I often mask emotional reactions in social settings and attempt to regulate my behaviour externally. This masking allows me to function in different situations and is a form of high-functioning adaptation. At the same time, I fear that a formal diagnosis could be used to suggest incapability or reduced competence, so I carefully manage what others see.

Why Do I Struggle to Stay Emotionally Connected?

One experience is difficulty maintaining emotional connection when people are not physically present. If someone is not actively in my life, the emotional connection can fade quickly. I may forget to reach out or maintain contact, even with people I care about.

Research on BPD shows that disruptions in attachment and object constancy are common [3]. Early relational trauma can lead to difficulty maintaining stable internal representations of others, which affects long-term emotional continuity in relationships [3]. Because past experiences created expectations of disappointment or abandonment, my brain sometimes anticipates rejection before relationships even develop.

Why Do My Feelings About People Change So Fast?

Someone I care about one day may feel distant, disliked, or irrelevant the next. In BPD research this pattern is often described as “splitting,” where individuals alternate between idealising and devaluing others [4]. Studies show that people with BPD often process interpersonal information in more extreme ways, which can lead to sudden shifts in emotional evaluation of others [5].

When I enter a fight-or-flight state, my thinking becomes rigid and polarised. Situations appear entirely good or entirely bad. However, when I feel calm, I tend to evaluate people and situations with more nuance. Emotional dysregulation is considered a core feature of BPD. Neuroimaging studies suggest that heightened amygdala activation combined with reduced regulatory control from the prefrontal cortex can produce intense emotional reactions and difficulty integrating complex interpretations during stress [2].

Why Does My Sense of Self Shift?

My sense of identity can also shift depending on who I am with. I often feel like different versions of myself appear in different contexts. People may only see one side of my personality rather than the whole person. Identity disturbance is another recognised feature of BPD. Research shows that individuals with the disorder often report unstable self-concepts and fluctuating roles or identities, partly because their sense of self developed in unstable relational environments [6].

Why Do Small Triggers Feel Huge?

I react strongly to small triggers compared with what others might experience. Minor interpersonal stressors can produce intense emotional responses. This heightened sensitivity is linked to emotional hyperreactivity, which is widely documented in BPD research [7][8].

How Has Trauma Affected My Personality?

My personality has also been deeply impacted by complex life experiences, including trauma. Personality disorders influence patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaviour across time. This means certain reactions may appear irrational or excessive. At times I may overshare personal information, while at other times I withdraw completely. Attachment research shows that people with trauma histories may alternate between anxious and avoidant attachment strategies depending on perceived threat or rejection [9].

How Did Medication Affect My Mood?

At one point I was prescribed Sertraline. After starting the medication, my mood shifted in a way that felt very different from my usual emotional patterns. I became unusually confident and more impulsive with decisions and spending. The increase in energy and behavioural change resembled Hypomania, a mood state associated with Bipolar Disorder [3].

Why Do My Emotions Feel So Intense?

Emotions often feel unusually intense. A romantic interest can dominate my thoughts and influence my mood for an entire day. Anger can feel physical, as though it spreads through my whole body. Even small negative interactions can trigger strong distress or hopeless thoughts [7][10].

Why Do I Act Impulsively?

Impulsive behaviours sometimes emerge as coping responses during intense emotional states. These behaviours are often attempts to regulate distress rather than deliberate decisions [11].

Why Do I Feel Suspicious or On Edge?

At times I become preoccupied with the idea that other people are judging me, criticising me privately, or intending to betray me. Research shows that individuals with BPD demonstrate heightened interpersonal threat sensitivity, meaning ambiguous social cues can be interpreted as rejection or hostility [8].
